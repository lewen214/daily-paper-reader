BOUNDEDNESS OF LEFT HALF-PLANE EIGENVALUES FOR NON-SELFADJOINT INDEFINITE STURM–LIOUVILLE PROBLEMS WITH APPLICATIONS TO FOURIER MODAL METHODS                 

> EHSAN FAGHIHIFAR ∗
> Abstract. We study a general class of non-selfadjoint indefinite Sturm–Liouville problems of the form
> −(p y ′)′+q y =λ p y,
> on a finite interval with complex-valued coefficients, where pis piecewise in W2,∞, non-vanishing, and satisfies a non-degenerate interface condition, and qis bounded. We prove that all eigenvalues in the open left half-plane are contained in a bounded set, which, by classical Sturm–Liouville theory, implies their finiteness. A prominent instance of this class arises in the lamellar grating diffraction problem with transverse-magnetic (TM) polarization, where p=ϵ(x)−1is the inverse of a spatially varying permittivity profile. Our result provides a simple and rigorous criterion for identifying non-physical spurious modes in low-loss metallic gratings—a notorious source of instability in Fourier modal methods. Numerical examples illustrate the practical utility of the criterion.
> Key words. Non-selfadjoint Sturm–Liouville problems, left half-plane eigenvalues, eigenvalue boundedness, Fourier modal methods, lamellar gratings, spurious modes

1. Introduction. The analysis of wave propagation and diffraction in periodic or layered media is a central problem in modern photonics. Lamellar gratings, as one-dimensional periodic structures, are particularly significant, both as canonical numerical models and key photonic components [77, 10, 2]. Among various numerical techniques devised to address this problem, modal methods are especially promi-nent, owing to their physical insight and computational efficiency [94, 85, 57, 49]. These methods rely on expressing the electromagnetic fields as a superposition of eigenmodes of a Sturm–Liouville (SL) differential operator arising from Maxwell’s equations, whose spectral properties, such as selfadjointness, completeness, eigen-value distribution, and boundedness, form the mathematical foundation of the frame-work [97, 38]. Early applications of modal methods in grating analysis relied on Fourier series to describe permittivity functions and electromagnetic fields [15, 42, 48]. Later, the analytic modal method (AMM) introduced a new approach for gratings with piece-wise constant permittivity profiles, determining layer eigenmodes in closed form and enforcing the interface conditions analytically [11, 13, 12, 14, 84, 54]. While concep-tually straightforward, AMM is restricted to simple geometries and requires solving transcendental characteristic equations in the complex plane. Subsequently, further numerical modal methods were developed. Most notably, inspired by the early Fourier modal implementations and an equivalent approximate method, called the coupled-wave method [50, 65], a more rigorous, generalized, standard Fourier-based modal approach took shape, known as the rigorous coupled-wave analysis (RCWA) or the Fourier modal method (FMM) [71, 69, 70, 73, 57, 58, 59]. To date, FMM remains a popular approach, owing to its physical insight, exceptional versatility, and computa-tional efficiency. Nonetheless, modal methods and particularly FMM, exhibit well-known far-field convergence difficulties for transverse-magnetic (TM) polarization in metallic grat-ings, in stark contrast to the well-behaved transverse-electric (TE) case [63]. Al-though the advent of Fourier factorization rules [33, 53, 56] helped circumvent trun-

Depending on implementation, these numerical anom-alies have been attributed to a variety of seemingly distinct but cognate sources, including the Gibbs phenomenon [63, 47], TCE [56, 53], ill-conditioning of Fourier coefficient matrices [78], violation of Li’s inverse rule [78, 92, 44], field singularities at metal–dielectric edges [37, 62, 61, 66], omission of ghost modes [29], and most no-tably, the emergence of spurious modes with unbounded propagation constants [64]. To that effect, numerous remedies have been proposed, including finite-difference or adaptive-resolution schemes [52, 30, 32, 35], subsectional spectral or pseudo-spectral approaches [74, 21, 22, 82, 17, 87, 86, 31], least-squares filtering [37, 36], artificial loss [78], and non-modal Fourier methods [45, 43]. Notwithstanding, fundamental questions regarding the nature of spurious modes remain largely unanswered. While regarded as numerical artifacts, the noted difficulties are also rooted in the spectral structure of the underlying differential operator, namely a non-selfadjoint indefinite Sturm–Liouville equation. Several studies have shown that the associated spectrum exhibits unexpected features, such as modes with propagation constants exceeding the bound typically associated with the maximum dielectric constant [90, 29], and ghost modes with non-real squared propagation constants (i.e., eigenvalues) even in lossless settings [29, 84, 89, 88]. Neglecting these modes leads to pronounced convergence defects [29], suggesting that such modes might even be infinite in number. Furthermore, because of the indefinite inner product, TM eigenmodes do not form a Hilbert space; consequently, modal completeness relies on a biorthogonal system, as established in non-selfadjoint spectral theory [49, 13, 38, 76]. A general SL problem over a finite interval seeks nontrivial solutions to (1.1) −(p y ′)′ + q y = λ w y, 

where p, q, w are complex-valued functions, y is the eigenfunction, and λ is the eigen-value. The TM problem in gratings lies in this framework, with the magnetic field component Hy as the eigenfunction, p = w = ϵ(x)−1, q = k20 , and λ = −β2, where k0

is the vacuum wave number, β is the longitudinal propagation constant, and ϵ(x) is the permittivity profile, i.e., typically modeled as a piecewise constant periodic func-tion which can change sign for metallic gratings and become complex-valued in the presence of losses. A similar formulation applies to TM modes in waveguides [49]. Allowing the potential q to be complex while keeping p > 0 and w > 0 (a.e.) can lead to complex eigenvalues [40]. If p changes sign while w > 0 (a.e.), the spectrum remains real under the associated Hilbert space framework, but can become unbounded from both below and above, provided the subsets on which p is positive and negative have nonzero Lebesgue measure [75]. A similar unboundedness result holds if w changes sign, under additional assumptions [51]. In the case of w, the sign change is the characteristic condition for the classical indefinite SL problems [97, 93]. Here, Kre˘ ın space theory provides a natural framework for the analysis [19]. Beyond the definite setting, spectral properties can indicate additional complex-ities. More recent works have established apriori bounds on their real and imaginary parts under various regularity assump-tions [4, 5, 6, 7, 80, 81, 46, 95, 96]. In the sense of the Riesz basis property , i.e., whether the eigenfunctions of the indefinite SL operator form a Riesz basis for the associated Hilbert space with |w| as the weight function, the outcome can be non-trivial. While under certain conditions completeness is proven [41, 3, 18], the Riesz basis property does not hold in general [91], and explicit counterexamples have been constructed [79, 27, 1, 8]. In the TM metallic grating setting, the governing equation falls into a less-explored regime of SL problems where both p and w may change sign, and may also be complex-valued. The resulting non-selfadjoint and indefinite nature of the operator alters analytic properties such as eigenvalue distribution, while also affecting numerical stability in modal computations. Motivated by the TM problem, we consider equation (1.1) and establish bound-edness of eigenvalues for a relatively general class in which p = w is piecewise in 

W 2,∞, non-vanishing, and admits finitely many discontinuities, while q is bounded. We further assume flux continuity together with a non-degenerate interface condition, and impose pseudo-periodic, Dirichlet, or Neumann boundary conditions. We show that, unlike standard indefinite problems and more in line with the definite setting, these assumptions imply that the eigenvalues are bounded in the left half-plane, from which classical Sturm–Liouville theory yields that only finitely many eigenvalues can lie in this region. From a computational photonics perspective, this result formalizes a long-standing numerical observation in Fourier modal methods and provides a simple yet rigorous criterion for distinguishing spurious modes. In the TM lamellar grating problem, this can be summarized as follows: as the truncation order increases, modes with Re( β2) → +∞ are identified as spurious and have no physical analogue. 2. Physical Motivation. In this work, gratings are non-magnetic media, typi-cally piecewise constant, that are periodic in the transverse plane and longitudinally invariant. In the context of gratings, eigenvalue problems arise in two distinct for-mulations of the same governing equation, each associated with different spectral properties. Consider the general vectorial wave equation for the electric or magnetic fields (2.1) ∇ × ∇ × E = k20 ϵE, ∇ × (ϵ−1∇ × H) = k20 H.

where k0 = ω/c is the vacuum wave number for ω denoting the angular frequency and c the speed of light. When the wave propagation is assumed in the transverse plane and (2.1) is solved for the frequency as the eigenvalue with given transverse components, e.g., in photonic band structure calculations, the resulting eigenvalue problem is selfadjoint. However, when the propagation has a longitudinal component, the transverse components and frequency are fixed, and (2.1) is solved for the longi-tudinal propagation constant, e.g., in diffraction analyses, selfadjointness holds only in special instances. This paper studies the latter case for lamellar gratings.

Remark 3.3 (Finiteness of eigenvalues in the left half-plane). The spectral clas-sification of regular two-point Sturm–Liouville boundary value problems given in [97, Lemma 3.2.4] implies that the eigenvalue set is either finite or countably infinite without finite accumulation points in C, except in the degenerate case where every complex number is an eigenvalue. In the present setting, Theorem 3.1 ensures that all eigenvalues with Re( λ) < 0 are contained in a bounded subset of C. Since no finite accumulation points occur in the non-degenerate cases, it follows that only finitely many eigenvalues can lie in the open left half-plane.

5. Conclusion. We establish a left half-plane boundedness result for the eigen-values of a class of non-selfadjoint indefinite SL problems. TM diffraction in lamellar gratings provides a physically important instance of this framework. The result im-plies that only finitely many eigenvalues may lie in the open left half-plane, thereby providing a criterion for identifying spurious modes in numerical eigenvalue solvers, particularly in Fourier modal methods. The accompanying numerical examples illus-trate the practical applicability of the result.
